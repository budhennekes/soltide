# Soltide science notes

Sourced research behind the in-app education. Confidence tags: **solid** (peer-reviewed consensus), **rule-of-thumb** (popular heuristic, plausible, not precisely validated), **shaky** (repeated but weakly supported). Rule for microcopy: build claims around solid mechanisms; hedge rule-of-thumb numbers as "aim for roughly."

## Lux / brightness (solid)
- Direct summer sun midday: ~100,000 lux. Full daylight (not direct): ~10,000 to 25,000. Overcast: ~1,000 to 10,000. Sunrise/sunset: ~400. Bright office/indoor: ~300 to 500.
- Headline true contrast: outdoor light is roughly 10x to 1,000x brighter than indoor light. (Wikipedia Daylight; EngineeringToolbox; EN 12464.)

## Morning light (solid mechanism)
- Light on the retina sets the SCN master clock, which gates melatonin. Morning light (after natural wake) phase-advances the clock, shifting melatonin onset earlier so you fall asleep earlier. (Nayak PNAS 2022 PMC9907124; Crowley & Eastman PMC4344919.)
- Dim-to-bright morning light raises cortisol >50%, helping the morning alertness pulse. (Scheer & Buijs JCEM 2001.)
- Melanopsin ipRGCs peak ~480 to 485 nm (blue). (PMC9907124.)
- Brightness beats duration: the first few minutes of bright light do most of the work; returns fall off fast. Accurate framing: "a few minutes of bright outdoor light beats a long time in dim indoor light." (St Hilaire PMC3406393.)
- Hedge as rule-of-thumb: exact minute counts (~5 clear / ~10 cloudy / ~20-30 overcast, Huberman), the "30-60 min after waking" window, the "16-hour timer." Safety (solid): look toward brightness, never directly at the sun. Do not tell users to remove medically needed eyewear.

## Evening light / phase delay (solid mechanism)
- Light's effect depends on timing (phase-response curve). Evening light before core-body-temperature minimum delays the clock (later sleep); morning light after it advances. Crossover ~CBTmin. (Khalsa J Physiol 2003 PMC2342968; St Hilaire 2012 PMC3406389; Duffy & Czeisler 2009.)
- Real-world evening room light (~200 lux) suppresses melatonin >50% in 85% of trials and shortens melatonin duration ~90 min. (Gooley JCEM 2011.)
- Light-emitting eReader 4h before bed: ~55% melatonin suppression, ~1.5h phase delay, ~10 min slower to fall asleep. (Chang PNAS 2015.)
- Blue/short-wavelength light suppresses more per lux (direction solid; exact % rule-of-thumb). Warm/dim evening light helps. Honest magnitude: "tens of minutes to ~2h delay depending on brightness, timing, duration."

## Melatonin suppression dose-response (solid)
- It's a curve, not a switch. Population half-max suppression somewhere ~25 to 100 lux depending on study (Zeitzer 2000 ~106 lux PMC2270041; Phillips 2019 ~25 lux PMC6575863); near-saturation by ~200 lux (typical room light).
- Headline teaching point: people differ >50-fold in light sensitivity (individual half-max ranged ~6 to 350 lux). There is no universal "safe" evening brightness. (Phillips PNAS 2019.)

## UV index (solid, EPA/WHO)
- Scale 0-11+. Categories & colors: 0-2 Low (green), 3-5 Moderate (yellow), 6-7 High (orange), 8-10 Very high (red), 11+ Extreme (violet). (WHO Global UV Index; US EPA.)
- UV is highest around solar noon, roughly 10am-4pm; near zero at night. Clouds usually reduce it but thin cloud can still pass substantial UV. Altitude raises it (~ +10-12% per 1,000m); snow reflects up to ~80%, water/sand less. (WHO/EPA.)
- Protection generally advised at UV 3+. Framing: color = signal, higher = protect skin/eyes sooner.

## Vitamin D (frame as wellness, never dosing)
- Skin makes vitamin D from UV-B specifically (~290-315 nm). UV-B only reaches the ground when the sun is high enough, so the window is the middle of the day, not early/late. (Standard photobiology; Holick.)
- Common rule-of-thumb thresholds: sun elevation above ~45 degrees, or UV index >= 3, as a practical "UV-B likely present" proxy. Treat as rule-of-thumb, not a clinical guarantee.
- "Vitamin D winter": at higher latitudes in winter the sun never gets high enough for meaningful UV-B, so skin can't make much vitamin D outdoors regardless of time spent. (Solid, latitude-dependent.)
- Skin type (Fitzpatrick) changes synthesis time: more melanin needs more time for the same amount. (Solid direction; exact times vary widely, keep vague.)
- Hard rule: no serum-level or IU dosing claims. Wellness disclaimer stays.

## General circadian (solid)
- The circadian rhythm is a ~24h internal cycle run by the SCN in the hypothalamus. External cues that set it are "zeitgebers"; light is the dominant one. Consistency of timing matters as much as any single day. Darkness at night is as important to the rhythm as light in the day.
